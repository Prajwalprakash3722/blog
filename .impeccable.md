## Design Context

### Users

**Primary audience**: A mixed tech community — developers, designers, PMs, and curious people in adjacent fields. They find posts through Twitter, search engines, and word of mouth. They're reading in varied contexts: late-night deep dives into a technical problem, casual discovery of a new voice in tech, or following a link someone shared because the take was interesting.

**Secondary audience**: Non-tech readers drawn to the philosophy, travel, and life writing. Friends, colleagues, people who know Prajwal and want to keep up with what he's thinking about.

**Reading context**: Often dark rooms, phones and laptops at night, but also coffee-break browsing during the day. The interface must work in both — readability is non-negotiable.

### Brand Personality

**Voice**: Warm, nerdy, candid — like talking to a sharp friend who happens to be an SRE and has strong opinions about everything from distributed systems to philosophy.

**Three words**: **Opinionated. Playful. Dense.**

The blog doesn't simplify for the audience — it trusts them. It has personality baked into small details (binary post count, vim cursor trail, "devcoffee" as identity). It's unafraid of strong takes, nerdy humor, and experimental touches. But under the playfulness is genuine substance — real technical depth, real personal writing.

**Emotional goals**: Visitors should feel like they stumbled into someone's carefully curated space — not a template, not a portfolio, but an actual *place* where someone thinks out loud. It should feel lived-in, a little surprising, and distinctly Prajwal.

### Anti-references

- **No generic dev blogs**: No Medium clones, no Hashnode/Dev.to templates, nothing that looks like 10,000 other developer blogs
- **No corporate polish**: No Vercel-template vibes, no startup landing page energy, no impersonal sleekness
- **No maximalist chaos**: No brutalist experiments, no 90s web nostalgia that sacrifices readability for style
- **No empty minimalism**: No plain-text-on-white blogs that feel like they gave up on design entirely

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme**: Dark mode as default identity, with openness to a refined light mode alternative if it serves readability. The dark isn't decorative — it's contextual (night readers, engineers in dark rooms, code-adjacent content).

**Visual tone**: A space that feels like a well-organized desk at 2am — dark, warm, focused, with small touches of personality. Think: the comfort of a good terminal color scheme meets the craft of an independent magazine. Not cold or clinical. Not flashy or attention-seeking. *Intentional*.

**Typography direction**: Move away from Inter (the current default). Find something with more character — a typeface pairing that reflects the blog's mix of technical precision and personal warmth. The display face should have opinion; the body face should be effortlessly readable in long-form.

**Color direction**: Evolve beyond the current purple-to-pink gradient palette (which reads as generic AI design). Find a palette rooted in the "devcoffee" identity — warm, grounded, with accents that feel deliberate rather than decorative. Tint neutrals toward the brand hue. Reserve accent color for moments that earn it.

**Details to preserve/evolve**:
- The binary post count is charming — keep some version of this nerdy humor
- The vim cursor trail is a signature — refine rather than remove
- The "one dark" code theme works well with a dark interface — maintain code block quality
- Glitch hover effects add personality but should be used sparingly

### Accessibility Requirements

**Target**: WCAG 2.1 AA compliance as a baseline. This is a personal commitment, not just a checkbox.

**Specifics**:
- Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for body text, 3:1 for large text and UI elements
- Full keyboard navigation support
- Semantic HTML structure with proper heading hierarchy
- Screen reader compatibility — meaningful alt text, ARIA labels where needed
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — all animations should have reduced-motion alternatives
- Focus indicators that are visible and styled intentionally (not hidden, not the default browser outline)
- Touch targets minimum 44×44px on interactive elements

### Design Principles

1. **Personality is in the details, not the decoration.** A vim cursor trail says more about who made this than a gradient ever could. Every visual choice should reflect Prajwal's actual interests and sensibility — not a "cool developer aesthetic" template.

2. **Readability is the first feature.** This is a blog. People come to read. Typography, contrast, line length, and spacing are not afterthoughts — they're the primary design surface. If a design choice hurts readability, it's wrong.

3. **Trust the reader.** Don't oversimplify, don't over-explain, don't add visual training wheels. The audience is smart and curious. Dense content with clear hierarchy beats shallow content with heavy visual scaffolding.

4. **Earn every element.** If something is on the page, it should have a reason. No decorative cards, no filler sections, no "looks designed" patterns that add nothing. Restraint is a feature.

5. **Accessibility is craft.** Making the blog work for everyone isn't a constraint on design — it's evidence of good design. The same care that goes into choosing a typeface should go into ensuring it works for a screen reader.
