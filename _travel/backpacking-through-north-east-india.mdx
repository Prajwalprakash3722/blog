# Backpacking Through North East India

I do not think North East India is the kind of place you should travel with too much confidence.

That sounds negative, but I mean it in the best way.

You can make a plan.

You can open Google Maps.

You can save cafes, hostels, waterfalls, monasteries, viewpoints, and every "must visit" reel that Instagram throws at you.

And then the hills will look at your plan and politely ignore it.

Rain will arrive.

A shared taxi will leave when it is full, not when your spreadsheet says it should.

Network will disappear exactly when you need to call someone.

Some road will be under repair.

Some stranger will tell you about a place you had never heard of, and suddenly the entire day will change.

That is the beauty of backpacking through North East India.

It does not let you fully control the trip.

And honestly, that might be the point.

## Why North East?

I had wanted to see North East India for a long time.

Not because of one specific place.

Not because I had a perfect itinerary.

Mostly because it felt different from the version of India I had grown up seeing.

Every photo looked like it belonged to another country.

Green hills.

Clouds sitting very low.

Roads bending into valleys.

Small towns with football grounds.

Rivers that looked too clean to be real.

And names that already sounded like travel:

```text
Shillong
Cherrapunji
Dawki
Kaziranga
Tawang
Ziro
Majuli
Gangtok
```

Even before going, the map itself felt like an invitation.

But I did not want to travel through it like a checklist.

I wanted to backpack.

Which is a fancy way of saying:

```text
carry less
book less
figure out more on the way
```

Sounds romantic.

Also sounds slightly stupid when you are standing in the rain with a wet bag.

Both can be true.

## The first lesson: distance is a lie

The first thing North East teaches you is that distance on the map means nothing.

In a city, if something is 80 km away, your brain starts calculating time with too much confidence.

In the hills, 80 km can be half a day.

Sometimes more.

The road is not just road.

It is:

```text
road
turn
hill
fog
truck
landslide warning
tea stop
driver waiting for one more passenger
another turn
```

You stop measuring travel in kilometres.

You start measuring it in patience.

That was hard for me in the beginning.

I am used to optimizing things.

Fastest route.

Best stay.

Cheapest cab.

Maximum places in minimum time.

North East does not reward that mindset much.

It rewards surrender.

Not spiritual surrender bro.

Just the normal travel version:

```text
the road will take as long as it takes
```

Once I accepted that, the trip became much better.

## Shared taxis are the real public transport

If you backpack through many parts of North East India, shared taxis become part of the story very quickly.

Not the polished airport taxi kind.

The real kind.

The kind where you ask around, find the stand, wait for the vehicle to fill, negotiate a little, put your bag somewhere, and hope your seat is not the one that teaches you about back pain.

There is something interesting about shared taxis.

They are uncomfortable, but they also make the trip feel alive.

You are sitting with people who actually live there.

Someone is going home.

Someone is carrying vegetables.

Someone knows the driver.

Someone gives unsolicited but useful advice.

Someone tells you the road ahead is bad.

Someone asks where you are from.

And slowly, the ride becomes less like transport and more like a moving waiting room where everyone knows a little more than you.

That is useful when you are backpacking.

Because half the time, the internet is not your best guide.

The person sitting next to you is.

## Meghalaya felt like the sky had come down

If there is one image that stays in my head from Meghalaya, it is clouds.

Not clouds far away.

Clouds right there.

On the road.

Between trees.

Around houses.

Sometimes it felt like the sky had come down and decided to walk with everyone.

Shillong had the energy of a hill town that was not trying too hard to impress anyone.

Cafes.

Music.

Football jerseys.

Traffic.

Students.

Cold evenings.

That mix of calm and chaos that makes a place feel lived in.

Then you move towards Cherrapunji or Sohra, and the landscape changes.

The roads open up.

The green becomes aggressive.

Waterfalls start appearing like the hills are leaking from every side.

And if you go during or around monsoon, the rain does not feel like weather.

It feels like a character in the trip.

Sometimes annoying.

Sometimes beautiful.

Always present.

## Dawki, rivers, and the problem with viral places

Some places become famous because of one photo.

Dawki is one of those names people usually know because of the clear river photos.

You see the image online and think:

```text
I need to see this once.
```

And fair enough.

The river is beautiful.

But viral places come with a strange problem.

Your expectations reach before you do.

You are not just seeing a place.

You are comparing it against a photo that was probably taken in perfect light, perfect season, perfect angle, and perfect water level.

That is not the place's fault.

That is our problem.

I had to remind myself of this multiple times on the trip.

Do not punish a place because Instagram edited your expectations.

Sometimes the real thing is quieter.

Sometimes messier.

Sometimes more crowded.

Sometimes better in a way that does not fit inside the photo you came for.

## Food, tea, and small stops

Backpacking makes you grateful for small food moments.

Not every meal has to be legendary.

Sometimes the best meal is just hot food after a cold ride.

Sometimes it is tea at a roadside stall while your shoes are still wet.

Sometimes it is whatever vegetarian option you can find without making the situation too complicated.

Food on trips like this becomes less about restaurants and more about timing.

You are hungry.

You are tired.

There is a small place open.

The food is hot.

That is enough.

I think travel makes ordinary things taste better because the body has earned them.

Tea after a long road.

Maggie in cold weather.

Rice after skipping lunch.

A packet of chips that becomes emergency dinner.

Beautiful cuisine reviews can happen later.

In the moment, survival food also has its own poetry.

## The backpack changes how you move

There is something very honest about carrying your own bag everywhere.

You learn very quickly what you actually need.

Extra clothes sound useful until you have to carry them uphill.

Backup shoes sound smart until they take half your bag.

Random "just in case" items slowly become personal enemies.

Backpacking makes you practical.

Not in a motivational quote way.

In a very physical way.

Your shoulder starts giving feedback.

Your back starts reviewing your life choices.

And suddenly minimalism makes sense.

The best packing advice is not philosophical.

It is simple:

```text
if you carry it long enough,
you will know whether you needed it
```

## People make the trip less lonely

The best part of backpacking is that you are alone, but not really.

You meet people in taxis.

You meet people in hostels.

You meet people at tea stalls.

You meet people who have just come from the place you are going.

You meet people going the opposite direction, carrying information like weather reports with legs.

They tell you:

```text
do not go there today
that road is bad
stay one more night here
leave early
carry cash
network is bad after this
that waterfall is not worth it
that village is beautiful
```

Some advice is useful.

Some advice is random.

Some advice contradicts the previous advice.

But the conversations themselves become part of the trip.

That is something I like about backpacking.

You become more available to the world.

If you travel with everything pre-booked and sealed, fewer things can go wrong.

But fewer things can enter too.

## The hard parts

I do not want to make the trip sound like one long montage.

Backpacking through North East India can be tiring.

The roads can be long.

The weather can mess with everything.

Internet can be unreliable.

Cash can matter more than you expect.

Some routes need permits.

Some places are not easy to reach.

Some days are just waiting.

Waiting for a vehicle.

Waiting for rain to slow down.

Waiting for clothes to dry.

Waiting for your phone to catch network.

Waiting for your body to stop being dramatic.

And because the region is huge, you also have to accept that you cannot see everything.

This is not a weekend checklist destination.

North East India is not one place.

It is many states, many cultures, many languages, many landscapes, and many different travel difficulties placed under one convenient label.

So if you try to "cover North East" in one trip, you will mostly cover exhaustion.

Better to choose a smaller route and actually feel it.

## What I would tell someone planning it

Do not overpack.

Carry cash.

Keep buffer days.

Start early.

Respect local rules and permit requirements.

Do not trust travel time blindly.

Do not turn every place into content.

Ask locals, but ask more than one person.

Expect rain, bad roads, and changed plans.

And most importantly:

```text
do not treat North East India like one destination
```

Meghalaya is not Arunachal.

Arunachal is not Assam.

Assam is not Nagaland.

Nagaland is not Sikkim.

Every state has its own rhythm.

The mistake is thinking one trip can explain all of it.

It cannot.

It can only introduce you.

## What I took back

Backpacking through North East India made me slower.

That is probably the simplest way to say it.

I went in wanting views, routes, photos, and stories.

I got those.

But the thing I remember more is the pace.

Waiting for shared taxis.

Watching fog move across a road.

Drinking tea because there was nothing else to do.

Changing plans because the weather did not care.

Talking to strangers because they knew more than the map.

Carrying less because my back had opinions.

Some trips make you feel like you conquered something.

This was not that kind of trip.

North East India does not need conquering.

It needs time.

It needs patience.

It needs respect.

And maybe a backpack that is lighter than the one you proudly packed on day one.
