# Leaving My First House

I am moving out of my house.

My first house.

The first place I moved into alone.

It has been around one and a half years. Now that I am packing, I keep looking at small things and remembering when I bought them.

Because for one and a half years, this place saw me every day.

It saw me wake up late.

It saw me wake up early.

It saw me cook a lot of food.

It saw me sit silently after long days.

It saw me do nothing and still feel tired.

When I first moved in, I think I was more excited about the idea of living alone than the actual reality of it. Living alone sounds peaceful from outside. You get your own space. No one asks questions. No one tells you what to do. You can eat when you want, sleep when you want, clean when you want.

And yes, all of that is true.

But a house does not feel like yours on the first day.

At first it is just a place where your bags are kept.

Then slowly you buy one thing. A bed. A TV. A table. A chair. Some vessels. A small thing for the kitchen. Something for the wall. Something you did not need urgently, but bought because the place started feeling empty without it.

Slowly the house starts looking like your life.

Not in one big moment. Just little by little.

One day you look around and realize, okay, this is my place now.

But slowly you also realize the other side.

No one wakes you up.

No one reminds you to eat.

No one notices if you are not doing okay.

No one is coming to fix your routine for you.

You have to become the person who tells yourself, "Get up."

And that is harder than it sounds.

## Food

One big memory from this house is food.

I cooked a lot here.

Not fancy food. Mostly normal food. The kind of food you make because you are hungry and Swiggy is getting too expensive. Poha, rice, idli, upma, dal, random experiments, and sometimes full confidence with zero skill.

I was always a good cook :)

But cooking alone still teaches you something.

![Food I cooked in this house](/assets/blog/first-house/poha.jpg)

Slowly, cooking became less about food and more about taking care of myself.

There is something very grounding about making your own food. You cannot fake it. If you do not put salt, there is no salt. If you burn it, it is burnt.

At the end of the day, it is you and fire cooking the food. It humbles you.

Life gives feedback very directly in the kitchen.

![Another plate from the kitchen](/assets/blog/first-house/idli.jpg)

I think I learnt that in this house. Small things matter. Washing the plate after eating matters. Buying fresh vegetables every single day matters. I did not have a fridge, so there was no "whatever is left" backup plan. If I wanted to eat properly, I had to step out, buy what I needed, and cook.

I also shared food with my neighbours sometimes. Nothing big. Just some extra rice, some curry, something I had made a little more of. But those small exchanges made the place feel less lonely.

These are not big life lessons, but when you live alone, these small things become your life.

## Living Alone

Living alone teaches you many things, but not in a motivational quote way.

It teaches you by making you face yourself.

You realize you are your own enemy a lot of the time.

Not the world.

Not luck.

Not people.

You.

Your laziness. Your overthinking. Your bad sleep. Your phone addiction. Your ability to postpone one simple task until it becomes a problem. Your talent for knowing exactly what is good for you and still doing the opposite.

That was one of the biggest lessons of this house.

When there is no one around, you cannot blame anyone for how your day goes. If the room is messy, it is you. If the sink is full, it is you. If you slept at 3 AM and woke up feeling dead, again, it is you.

At first that feels irritating.

Then slowly it becomes freeing.

Because if you are the problem, maybe you can also become the solution.

Not every day. Not perfectly. But slowly.

## Leaving

Now I am leaving this house, and I do not know what exactly I feel.

I am not crying. I am not heartbroken. I am also not completely casual about it.

It is just one of those moments where you look around and realize a place has become part of your life without asking for permission.

This house was not perfect.

Some days it felt lonely.

Some days it felt like freedom.

Some days it was just four walls and a kitchen.

But it was mine for a while.

![A meal from this chapter](/assets/blog/first-house/rice.jpg)

I learnt to live here.

I learnt to cook here.

I learnt that discipline is not some big personality trait. Sometimes it is just waking up, washing your plate, making food, doing your work, sleeping at a decent time, and repeating it even when no one is watching.

I also learnt that independence is not always exciting. Sometimes it is boring. Sometimes it is lonely. Sometimes it is just you standing in the kitchen wondering what to eat.

But I am grateful for this house.

It gave me space.

It gave me silence.

It gave me a version of myself I had to meet properly.

And now I am moving out.

To a better house.

Still living alone.

Not with a big lesson.

Not with a perfect ending.

Just with some packed bags, a few food photos, and a quiet feeling that this place mattered.
