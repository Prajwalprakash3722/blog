# Live Captions at the Movies

Yesterday, I went to watch _Obsession_ at PVR Bengaluru.

It is a supernatural horror film by Curry Barker.

I read a little about it later, and apparently the movie has had a pretty crazy run. Low budget, word-of-mouth, people showing up, all that. I like when films like that work.

I liked the movie.

Actually, I liked it a lot.

The show did not have subtitles.

That part sucked.

I am deaf, so subtitles are not some bonus feature for me. They are basically how I watch the movie properly.

In India, subtitles for movies feel like a gamble. Not just English movies. Literally all movies.

Sometimes subtitles are there. Sometimes they are not. Sometimes the booking page does not make it clear enough. Sometimes you only find out after the movie starts and the first few lines are already gone.

Usually, that is enough to make the movie feel like work.

I have to guess things. Watch faces more carefully. Fill gaps. Hope I did not miss something important.

Not because the movie becomes impossible, but because it becomes work. I have to follow faces more closely. I have to fill gaps. I have to sit there while everyone else is simply watching, while I am doing some weird mental debugging of dialogue, expressions, background sound, and vibes.

It is awful man.

I am already in the theatre. I have paid for the ticket. I have bought popcorn and coke. And now instead of watching the movie properly, I am just sitting there eating popcorn and drinking coke while trying to understand what is happening.

That is such a stupid feeling.

I wrote about this before in [Is 'Accessibility' Losing Its Meaning?](/posts/accessibility-is-the-word-losing-its-meaning). Yesterday was one of those small real-life reminders of why I wrote that.

Then I remembered my iPhone has Live Captions.

So I turned it on.

And I watched the whole movie like that.

My phone was just sitting there, listening to the movie and trying to turn it into text in real time.

Was it perfect?

No.

Sometimes it lagged.

Sometimes it missed words.

Sometimes horror-movie whispering became some mysterious sentence that even the iPhone probably did not believe.

But it worked.

Enough.

And honestly, enough was good enough yesterday.

When I wrote [AI is Finally Listening](/posts/ai-is-finally-listening), I was thinking about this exact feeling. Not that technology has fixed everything. It has not. We are still far from that. Captions still lag. Accuracy still depends on accents, noise, clarity, and a hundred other things. Regional languages are still treated like optional side quests. And cinema halls still need to do better.

But yesterday felt like a tiny glimpse of the world I keep hoping for.

A world where I do not have to plan my life around whether captions exist.

A world where access is not luck.

I know it sounds dramatic to say a phone feature made me emotional.

Maybe someone reading this will think, bro, why are you making this such a big deal?

Fair.

From the outside, it probably looks like a small thing.

But if you stand in my shoes for a bit, it is not small.

When enough small things keep leaving you out, one small thing that includes you can feel huge.

The funny part is, I went in to watch a horror movie.

I came out thinking about accessibility.

Classic me, I guess.

But I also came out happy.

Because _Obsession_ was good.

And because my phone became the subtitle track the theatre did not give me.

That is not the ideal solution, obviously.

Movies should have captions. Theatres should care. Accessibility should not depend on whether my phone can rescue the experience.

But until that happens, I am glad something like Live Captions exists.

Yesterday was one of those days.

Nothing dramatic.

Just a movie.

No subtitles.

An iPhone trying its best.

And me being able to watch the whole thing.

Felt nice.
